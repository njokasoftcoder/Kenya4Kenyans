1

Fragmented Governance and Broken Coordination

Fellow Kenyans, we often speak of vision and ambition, yet too frequently our plans stumble long before they reach the people they are meant to serve. One of the most visible constraints is the way our government institutions operate in silos. For example, the Ministry of Lands has its own land management information system, while the Ministry of Agriculture, the National Treasury, and other agencies maintain separate databases that do not communicate with one another. Similarly, county information systems are often not linked to national databases. Each ministry and county collects and stores data independently, with little integration or coordination.

This lack of interconnected systems hinders the government's ability to process information efficiently, identify overlaps, or plan comprehensively. Vision 2030, the Medium Term Plans, BETA, and county development blueprints all exist, but their implementation suffers because departments cannot easily share data, track progress, or align initiatives. Projects stall, budgets are underutilised, and national planning becomes fragmented rather than strategic.

What stands in our way is not just policy gaps, but an absence of an integrated government information infrastructure. Ministries and counties may act diligently within their own mandates, but without seamless data sharing, coordination is slow, decisions are reactive rather than proactive, and resources are often wasted. Political negotiation and overlapping mandates compound the problem, slowing even technically sound projects.

Contrast this with Rwanda, where departments collaborate through integrated reporting systems, allowing leadership to see real-time progress, prevent duplication, and enforce accountability. Singapore achieves similar efficiency by linking agencies and databases, ensuring that all arms of government operate in a coordinated, informed manner. These examples show us that ambition without integrated information systems, and without coherent interdepartmental coordination, cannot yield results efficiently.

The Way Forward:

For Kenya to achieve rapid and sustainable progress, we must invest in a unified, interoperable government information system that links ministries, agencies, and counties. Such a system would allow real-time data sharing, coordinated planning, and efficient monitoring of project outcomes. Clear accountability, enforced performance, and technology-enabled integration will ensure that national visions are translated into tangible, timely, and impactful results for all Kenyans.

2

Individually Driven Agendas

Fellow Kenyans, another challenge that slows our progress is the way national development agendas are often driven by individuals rather than by collective vision. Too frequently, priorities are announced by political leaders or administrations without meaningful citizen participation. Citizens are consulted after plans are made, rather than being actively involved in shaping them. The result is a sense that development is something delivered to us, not something we co-create.

This approach limits Kenya in multiple ways. When policies belong to one leader or administration, they become fragile and vulnerable to reversal with each change of government. Public buy-in is low, and trust in the execution of projects is easily undermined. Citizens, feeling excluded, often disengage or resist reforms, slowing implementation further. Our democracy, which rightly values political leadership, struggles to translate that leadership into sustained national progress when citizen ownership is absent.

Look at Rwanda, where citizens are not just spectators but active participants in national priorities. Through structured dialogue and systems like Imihigo performance contracts, every leader is accountable to the people, and citizens are empowered to monitor progress.

In Singapore, civic engagement and education were central to the nation's transformation; citizens were informed, consulted, and made to feel part of the collective journey from poverty to prosperity. China, too, demonstrates the power of local and regional alignment: policies and economic plans are structured to balance central vision with local execution, allowing local administrations to implement national priorities while contributing feedback that strengthens outcomes.

The Path to Collective Ownership:

For Kenya to accelerate its development, we must embed citizen ownership at the heart of national planning. This means creating forums where citizens can help define priorities, understand trade-offs, and contribute to monitoring outcomes. It also means aligning counties and local administrations with national goals while giving them clear responsibility and accountability. Development will only be sustainable when the people of Kenya see themselves reflected in the policies and programs meant to serve them.

3

High Cost of Doing Business

Fellow Kenyans, economic growth is the lifeblood of development, yet in Kenya, the cost of doing business remains high and a significant barrier to progress. Entrepreneurs face multiple challenges: electricity tariffs that are unpredictable, numerous licenses and permits that often overlap, cumbersome regulatory compliance, and county fees that differ from one jurisdiction to another. Formalising a business can feel like punishment rather than opportunity. For small and medium enterprises, these costs often outweigh potential benefits, keeping them informal and limiting their growth.

This fragmented and expensive business environment suppresses innovation, restricts employment creation, and discourages both domestic and foreign investment. Unlike Kenya, Singapore prioritised a predictable and streamlined business environment. Businesses know exactly what is required to start and grow, and compliance is simplified and consistent. Rwanda reduced regulatory friction by streamlining licensing processes and linking registration to market access and business support. China, meanwhile, leveraged industrial clusters and special economic zones, providing infrastructure, financial support, and coordinated services, allowing firms to scale efficiently and compete globally.

In Kenya, the absence of similar coordination and predictability means that even the most ambitious businesses struggle to survive, let alone contribute to national transformation.
Creating a Business-Friendly Kenya:

To accelerate development, we must treat businesses as partners, not revenue targets. This requires simplified, harmonised regulations, predictable taxation, and incentives that encourage formalisation, innovation, and growth. We must also link county and national regulations to eliminate contradictions and duplication, creating an environment where businesses can thrive and contribute meaningfully to employment, wealth creation, and the economy.

By learning from Singapore's efficiency, Rwanda's streamlined processes, and China's strategic clusters, Kenya can create a business ecosystem that supports rapid, sustainable economic growth, reduces informality, and transforms entrepreneurial potential into real, measurable national progress.

4

Over-Taxation and Weak Social Contract

Fellow Kenyans, another obstacle to rapid development is the heavy taxation burden that citizens and businesses face, often without the promise of tangible benefits. We pay PAYE, VAT, excise duties, fuel levies, and numerous county fees ‚Äî yet essential public services like healthcare, schools, roads, and utilities remain underfunded or inefficient. This creates a paradox: we are taxed heavily, but still pay privately for services that should be guaranteed.

This situation undermines both productivity and trust. When citizens see their contributions failing to translate into improved services, compliance decreases, businesses hesitate to invest, and the social contract ‚Äî the implicit agreement that taxes support services in return ‚Äî is weakened. In essence, over-taxation without visible delivery discourages growth and slows national progress.

Look to Singapore, where taxation is predictable, transparent, and directly linked to visible services. Citizens understand what their contributions fund, and the government demonstrates results consistently, fostering trust and compliance. Rwanda also emphasises a clear link between taxes and public benefits, ensuring that every shilling contributes to infrastructure, healthcare, or education. China, while using different mechanisms, channels government revenue strategically into productive sectors, allowing investments to generate returns that justify the cost of taxes.

For Kenya, the lesson is clear: we must rebuild the social contract. Taxation should be fair, predictable, and directly linked to service delivery.
Rebuilding Trust Through Service Delivery:

Government must prioritise functional public hospitals, schools, and utilities, demonstrating that every shilling collected contributes to measurable improvements in the lives of citizens. When the people see value for their contributions, compliance rises, businesses grow, and the economy flourishes.

By aligning taxation with service delivery, Kenya can transform over-taxation from a burden into a tool for development, creating trust, productivity, and sustainable growth.

5

Informality Trap and Human Capital Waste

Fellow Kenyans, a large portion of our workforce operates in the informal sector, and many enterprises remain micro-scale, unable to access credit, insurance, or markets. At the same time, our education system, including the Competency-Based Curriculum (CBC), is not yet fully aligned with the needs of the economy or national priorities. While CBC aims to develop critical thinking, problem-solving, and life skills, it often falls short in providing the specialized, market-ready skills demanded by industries such as manufacturing, ICT, agro-processing, renewable energy, and the blue economy.

Moreover, the cost of accessing education remains prohibitively high for many families. Even with free primary and subsidized secondary education policies, hidden costs‚Äîuniforms, books, transport, private tuition, and examination fees‚Äîlimit equitable access and prevent many talented youth from reaching their potential. The combination of skills mismatch, high educational costs, and lack of accessible vocational training wastes human capital and slows national progress.

The consequences are severe: informality constrains economic scaling, reduces job creation, and limits innovation. Graduates struggle to find employment, businesses struggle to find skilled labor, and investors hesitate, knowing the talent pipeline is weak. Singapore addressed this by linking education directly to national economic priorities, ensuring both practical skills and civic competencies. Rwanda gradually aligned vocational training and formalisation incentives with sectors that drive economic growth. China integrated workforce development into industrial clusters, connecting education, employment, and enterprise.

For Kenya, we must break the informality trap and align human capital with economic needs.
Unlocking Our Human Potential:

This requires affordable, accessible education, stronger technical and vocational training, and curricula designed around the skills required by county and national economic priorities. By doing so, Kenya can unlock productivity, reduce human capital waste, and transform the potential of its people into tangible development outcomes.

6

Fiscal Discipline and Resource Mobilization

Fellow Kenyans, our nation's growth has long been constrained by the temptation to rely on external borrowing, often resulting in projects that are neither visible nor transformative. Too often, funds raised through debt end up tied to initiatives that never reach completion or fail to contribute meaningfully to national development ‚Äî what many have called "ghost projects."

Instead, Kenya can chart a different path: living within our means, mobilizing resources internally, and growing organically. By focusing on efficient collection of taxes, strengthening local revenue generation, and eliminating waste and duplication, we can fund projects that are tangible, impactful, and directly felt by citizens. Resources raised internally, when well managed, can be strategically deployed to priority sectors like industrial parks, technical training centers, and county-led economic initiatives.

Singapore, Rwanda, and China all demonstrate the power of disciplined, internally-driven growth.

Singapore transformed its economy not by borrowing heavily, but by creating efficient, productive systems and reinvesting domestic revenue strategically. Rwanda mobilized internal resources while maintaining strict fiscal discipline, ensuring that every shilling spent contributed to visible infrastructure, health, and education improvements. China prioritized industrial clusters and local development funded largely through domestic savings and productive investment, creating self-sustaining growth.

The Path to Sustainable Growth:

For Kenya, the lesson is clear: we must harness our own resources, manage them prudently, and invest in projects that deliver tangible results. By growing organically and living within our means, we can avoid the pitfalls of ghost projects, strengthen public trust, and create a sustainable foundation for rapid, meaningful, and inclusive development.

7

Bloated Governance and Fragmented Coordination

Fellow Kenyans, another challenge that slows our progress is the sheer size and inefficiency of our governance structures. Across national and county levels, we have a proliferation of ministries, departments, and agencies, many with overlapping mandates. Decision-making is slow, accountability is diffuse, and resources are often spread thinly across structures that contribute little to actual development outcomes.

Our level of representation is particularly striking. Relative to our GDP, Kenya maintains a large number of elected officials and administrators, far exceeding what is necessary to manage public affairs effectively. The cost of this over-representation consumes resources that could otherwise be invested directly in schools, hospitals, roads, and industries. Citizens often bear the burden of high administrative costs while seeing limited improvement in public services.

Compounding this is the prevalence of tokenistic gestures by political leaders: the opening of cattle dips, handing out water tanks, electricity transformers, or wheelbarrows framed as "empowerment" initiatives. While these gestures may win short-term political approval, they do little to drive structural economic growth or improve livelihoods sustainably. Resources spent on such performative acts could instead fund infrastructure, vocational training, or industrial development that creates lasting impact.

This fragmentation and performative governance prevent coherent planning and execution. Ministries and counties often operate in isolation, pursuing their own priorities rather than aligning with national goals.

Projects stall, duplication occurs, and the impact of public spending is diluted. Citizens feel the disconnect, losing trust in government's ability to deliver tangible improvements in their lives.

Contrast this with Singapore, where governance is lean, highly accountable, and performance-driven. Every agency has clear roles, minimal overlap, and measurable outcomes. Rwanda also streamlined its institutions, focusing resources on priority sectors while enforcing strict accountability through performance contracts. China, with its regional governance model, ensures that central policies are implemented locally with clarity, while local administrations remain accountable for results. These nations demonstrate that lean, coordinated, and accountable governance, proportionate to economic capacity, accelerates development, reduces waste, and builds citizen confidence.

Building Efficient Governance:

For Kenya, the path forward is clear: we must rationalize government structures, reduce over-representation, curb performative spending, clearly define roles and responsibilities, and eliminate duplication. Resources saved from bloated administration and tokenistic gestures should be redirected to high-impact projects and services that citizens can see and benefit from. By creating a lean, integrated governance system proportional to our economic capacity, Kenya can achieve faster, more visible, and sustainable development.

8

Weak Policy Implementation and Regulatory Friction

Fellow Kenyans, even when policies are well-intentioned, Kenya struggles with implementation and regulatory friction. Regulations are often complex, overlapping, or inconsistently enforced. Ministries, counties, and agencies frequently interpret rules differently, creating confusion for businesses, investors, and citizens alike. Projects that should proceed smoothly are delayed by bureaucratic bottlenecks, unclear procedures, and uncoordinated oversight. This weak implementation not only slows development but discourages innovation and formalisation.

The cumulative effect is a nation that has policies on paper but limited visible outcomes. Ambitious agendas like Vision 2030, BETA, and the Medium Term Plans often fail to translate into tangible improvements because the machinery of government cannot execute efficiently. Meanwhile, political leaders sometimes focus on performative acts‚Äîgroundbreakings, handouts, or ceremonial openings‚Äîrather than ensuring that the policies behind them deliver measurable results. The result is a gap between ambition and reality, leaving citizens frustrated and trust in government eroded.

Singapore, Rwanda, and China provide contrasting examples of how effective implementation can transform a nation.

Singapore's policies are accompanied by clear enforcement, streamlined processes, and performance accountability at every level. Rwanda uses Imihigo performance contracts to ensure that every official, from local administrators to national ministers, delivers on clearly defined targets. China aligns central directives with local execution, reducing regulatory friction and ensuring that policy translates into action on the ground. In all three countries, strong implementation transforms vision into measurable outcomes, earning citizen trust and sustaining growth.

From Policy to Practice:

For Kenya, the lesson is clear: policies are only as good as their execution. We must simplify regulations, clarify responsibilities, enforce compliance consistently, and remove bureaucratic bottlenecks. At the same time, we must prioritize substance over spectacle, ensuring that every initiative‚Äîwhether national or county-level‚Äîdelivers real, visible impact. By strengthening implementation and reducing regulatory friction, Kenya can bridge the gap between vision and reality, allowing citizens to experience tangible progress in their daily lives.

9

County Differentiation for Economic Priorities

Fellow Kenyans, our counties are not all the same, and neither should their development strategies be. Kenya has vast regional differences‚Äîcoastlines, arid frontiers, fertile highlands, and urban hubs‚Äîthat offer unique opportunities for growth. Yet, too often, we pursue uniform policies that fail to leverage local strengths, spreading resources thinly rather than building competitive advantage.

Take Mombasa, for example. With its deep-water port and strategic location on the Indian Ocean, it is uniquely positioned to lead Kenya's blue economy, including shipping, logistics, fisheries, and marine tourism. North Eastern Kenya, with its vast rangelands, is ideal for beef and leather industries, while Nairobi and the surrounding counties can focus on innovation, ICT, and financial services. Tailoring county development plans to local resources, skills, and infrastructure allows Kenya to unlock growth faster, rather than trying to apply a one-size-fits-all approach.

China provides a clear lesson in regional differentiation. Its Special Economic Zones and industrial clusters were deliberately aligned with local resources and strategic priorities, creating regional hubs that fuelled national growth.

Singapore, though small, demonstrates the importance of focusing efforts where comparative advantage exists, ensuring maximum impact from limited resources. Rwanda has also successfully concentrated development initiatives regionally, aligning infrastructure, human capital, and investment incentives with specific economic sectors.

Unlocking Regional Potential:

For Kenya, adopting a county-differentiated development model is essential. Counties must identify and develop sectors in which they have a comparative advantage, backed by targeted investments, skills development, and infrastructure. National policy and funding should support these priorities rather than diluting efforts across uniform programs. By doing so, Kenya can turn regional strengths into national prosperity, ensuring that every county contributes meaningfully to the country's transformation.

10

Strengthening Human Capital and Skills Alignment

Fellow Kenyans, our greatest resource is our people, yet too often, we fail to fully harness their potential. While the Competency-Based Education (CBC) curriculum aims to equip students with critical thinking, problem-solving, and life skills, it is still not fully aligned with the practical needs of our economy. Many graduates, despite their competencies, struggle to find meaningful employment because their skills do not match the demands of industries like manufacturing, ICT, the blue economy, agro-processing, and livestock and leather sectors.

Compounding this is the high cost of accessing education. Even with free primary and subsidized secondary education, families face hidden costs‚Äîbooks, uniforms, transportation, private tuition, and examination fees‚Äîthat prevent many talented youth from reaching their potential. The result is human capital waste, where a bright, energetic workforce is underutilized or trapped in low-productivity informal employment.

Singapore, Rwanda, and China demonstrate the power of aligning education with economic needs. Singapore linked its education system directly to national priorities, ensuring that students and graduates acquire market-ready skills. Rwanda aligned vocational and technical training with key sectors, creating a workforce that drives visible economic growth. China integrated technical skills, industry, and regional priorities, producing a pipeline of workers equipped to support industrial clusters and innovation hubs.

For Kenya, the path forward is clear: we must align education, vocational training, and higher learning with county and national economic priorities.
Building Our Future Workforce:

This means:

  • Making education affordable and accessible to all, eliminating hidden costs that block participation.
  • Strengthening Technical and Vocational Education and Training (TVET) institutions and linking them directly to industries with high growth potential.
  • Mapping skills to local and national economic priorities, so that every graduate contributes meaningfully to sectors like Mombasa's blue economy, North Eastern Kenya's beef and leather industries, and Nairobi's ICT and innovation hubs.
  • Encouraging partnerships between schools, colleges, businesses, and counties to create apprenticeships, internships, and practical learning opportunities.

By investing in skills alignment and affordable education, Kenya can transform human potential into measurable economic growth, reduce informality, and ensure that our youth are prepared to contribute to a modern, competitive, and prosperous nation.

11

Integrated Government Information Systems

Fellow Kenyans, a major barrier to efficient governance and rapid development is the fragmentation of our information systems. Ministries, departments, and counties often operate in silos, each maintaining its own databases and management systems. For example, the Ministry of Lands has a land management system that is not linked to other relevant ministries, while county systems often operate independently of national databases. This lack of integration prevents real-time data sharing, coordinated planning, and effective monitoring of national projects.

The consequences are clear: planning becomes fragmented, overlapping initiatives waste resources, and decision-making is slow and reactive rather than strategic. National policies, county development plans, and sectoral strategies exist, but their execution suffers because critical information is isolated, preventing the government from seeing the bigger picture and acting efficiently.

Singapore, Rwanda, and China provide instructive examples. Singapore links government agencies through integrated digital platforms, allowing real-time monitoring, data-driven decision-making, and rapid coordination across departments.

Rwanda has implemented national reporting and monitoring systems that enable leadership to track progress and enforce accountability. China integrates regional and national data systems, ensuring that local and central governments are aligned and that information flows seamlessly to support industrial and regional development.

Building a Connected Kenya:

For Kenya, the lesson is clear: we must develop a unified, interoperable government information system. Ministries, agencies, and counties should share data in real time, enabling coordinated planning, effective resource allocation, and performance tracking. Such integration will reduce duplication, improve service delivery, and allow citizens and investors to see measurable outcomes. By harnessing technology and breaking down informational silos, Kenya can transform governance from a fragmented and reactive system into one that is efficient, transparent, and capable of driving rapid national progress.

12

Strengthening Civic Participation and National Vision Ownership

Fellow Kenyans, a recurring challenge in our national development journey is the lack of meaningful citizen participation in shaping policies and priorities. Too often, development agendas are announced by political leaders without involving citizens in the decision-making process. This creates a disconnect: policies are perceived as top-down initiatives rather than shared national commitments, and public ownership of development outcomes is limited.

When citizens are excluded, policies become fragile, vulnerable to reversal with each change in government, and difficult to implement effectively. Public trust diminishes, and engagement is low, making it harder to achieve rapid, transformative results. Individually-driven agendas, performative gestures, and fragmented communication further weaken the collective sense of purpose.

Singapore, Rwanda, and China provide instructive examples. In Singapore, civic engagement and public education ensure that citizens understand, support, and participate in national priorities, creating a strong social contract that sustains long-term development.

Rwanda's Imihigo performance contracts link citizen oversight with leadership accountability, empowering communities to monitor progress and contribute to outcomes. China aligns local and regional implementation with central policy objectives, ensuring that citizen expectations, local priorities, and national vision work in concert.

Building a Kenya of, by, and for the People:

For Kenya, the lesson is clear: citizens must be central to the design, execution, and monitoring of national development agendas. This means creating forums for public consultation, embedding community oversight in project implementation, and aligning county and national priorities with the lived realities of citizens. Development will only be sustainable when every Kenyan sees themselves reflected in the vision, participates actively, and shares responsibility for outcomes. By strengthening civic participation and national vision ownership, Kenya can transform governance from a top-down exercise into a collective journey toward prosperity.

13

Lessons from Global Comparators and the Path Forward

Fellow Kenyans, as we chart the path toward transforming Kenya into a prosperous, globally competitive nation, we can draw critical lessons from countries that have successfully achieved rapid development. Singapore, Rwanda, and China demonstrate that deliberate planning, disciplined execution, citizen engagement, and strategic use of local advantages can transform a nation within a generation.

Singapore shows us the power of efficiency, accountability, and alignment. It built lean governance structures, streamlined business regulations, and aligned education and workforce development with economic priorities. Citizens understood and participated in the national vision, taxation was linked to visible public services, and policy implementation was rigorous.

Rwanda highlights citizen ownership and performance accountability. Through Imihigo contracts, communities participate in tracking development outcomes, ensuring that leaders deliver measurable results. Strategic prioritization and regional focus allow limited resources to have maximum impact.

China illustrates the benefits of regional differentiation and integrated planning. By developing local and regional industrial clusters aligned with national priorities, China leveraged unique local strengths while maintaining strong central coordination. This combination of decentralization with alignment ensured rapid economic scaling and reduced waste.

For Kenya, these lessons translate into a roadmap for sustainable, inclusive development.
Kenya's Transformation Roadmap:
  • Lean, accountable governance: Rationalize ministries and departments, reduce over-representation, curb tokenistic spending, and clearly define roles.
  • Integrated information systems: Link national and county databases, enabling real-time decision-making, monitoring, and coordination.
  • Fiscal discipline and internal resource mobilization: Fund projects within our means, eliminate ghost projects, and ensure every shilling contributes to visible, high-impact outcomes.
  • Economic competitiveness and county specialization: Align counties with their comparative advantages, such as Mombasa for the blue economy, North Eastern Kenya for beef and leather, and Nairobi for ICT and innovation.
  • Human capital alignment: Make education affordable, strengthen vocational and technical training, and ensure skills match economic priorities.
  • Regulatory efficiency and business-friendly environment: Streamline compliance, reduce bureaucracy, and create incentives for formalization and innovation.
  • Civic participation and national vision ownership: Engage citizens in shaping and monitoring development agendas, fostering trust and collective responsibility.

By following these principles, Kenya can turn its vast potential into tangible growth, transforming the economy, creating jobs, improving services, and ensuring prosperity reaches every corner of the country. Like Singapore, Rwanda, and China, we can achieve rapid, visible progress ‚Äî but only if we act deliberately, strategically, and inclusively.

Fellow Kenyans, the challenge is ours, but so too is the opportunity. By learning from others and committing to disciplined, citizen-centered development, we can build a Kenya that is modern, prosperous, and truly reflective of the dreams of its people.

A Call to Action

Fellow Kenyans, we stand at a pivotal moment in our nation's history. The vision of transforming Kenya into a prosperous, competitive, and inclusive country is within reach‚Äîbut only if we confront the challenges that have held us back for too long. Fragmented governance, bloated representation, regulatory friction, misaligned education, high taxation without delivery, informal economic traps, and tokenistic leadership have slowed our progress.

But we also see the possibilities. Lessons from Singapore, Rwanda, and China remind us that strategic planning, disciplined execution, citizen participation, and alignment of resources with local strengths can transform a nation within a generation. Kenya has the talent, the resources, and the potential; what remains is the collective will to act decisively, responsibly, and inclusively.

We call upon every Kenyan‚Äîleaders, policymakers, entrepreneurs, educators, and citizens‚Äîto take ownership of our national destiny. Let us demand accountability, support policies that are implemented efficiently, prioritize visible and impactful projects, and ensure that every Kenyan has the opportunity to contribute meaningfully to our country's growth.

This is not the work of one person, one party, or one county. It is the responsibility of all of us. By living within our means, investing in our people, leveraging the unique strengths of our regions, and building governance systems that are lean, transparent, and accountable, we can turn the dream of a modern, prosperous Kenya into a reality.

The path is clear, the lessons are available, and the opportunity is ours. Let us rise together, not for short-term applause, but for a Kenya that future generations will proudly inherit.

Fellow Kenyans, the time to act is now.
